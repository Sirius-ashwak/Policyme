Part 2: System Architecture & User Roles
2.1 The B2B2C Architecture Model
PolicyMe operates as a B2B2C (Business-to-Business-to-Consumer) platform[6]:
The "B2B" Layer (Your Customer):
Insurance carriers (Geico, State Farm, etc.) purchase PolicyMe as an enterprise SaaS subscription
PolicyMe is installed as an internal "Core Intelligence System" behind the carrier's firewall
Annual contract: $100K-$500K depending on claim volume
The "2C" Layer (The End Consumer):
John Doe (regular customer) crashes his car and files a claim via Geico's mobile app
John never sees PolicyMe's interface
The Geico mobile app sends claim data via API to PolicyMe's backend
PolicyMe processes the claim and returns decision to Geico's systems
John only sees: "Claim Approved" in his Geico app
Why This Separation Matters:
Security: Internal AI systems cannot be exposed directly to public internet
Liability: AI never tells customers directly—human adjusters verify first
Compliance: Sensitive medical/financial data stays behind enterprise firewall
This architectural separation is critical for your Infosys presentation. You are NOT building a consumer app; you are building the internal command center that insurance companies use to process consumer claims 10x faster.
2.2 Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) Matrix
PolicyMe implements enterprise-grade RBAC with four distinct user roles, each with specific permissions aligned with the principle of Least Privilege[7][8]:
Role 1: Insured Policyholder (External Consumer)
Persona: John Doe (car crash victim filing claim)
Access Method: Geico's customer mobile app (NOT PolicyMe's interface)
What They Can Do:
Submit new claims with photos and accident details
View status of their own claims ("Pending," "Under Review," "Approved")
Read their specific active policy documents
What They CANNOT Do:
See other customers' data
Access AI reasoning or GraphRAG backend
Approve or modify claim decisions
Technical Note: This role does NOT log into PolicyMe. Their data enters via API integration from the carrier's customer portal.

Role 2: Claims Adjuster (Internal Operations User)
Persona: Sarah (Geico employee processing claims)
Dashboard: InsurAI Command Center (Dark-mode, GraphRAG interface)
Access Permissions:
View incoming claims queue assigned to them
Access "Ask PolicyMe" AI interface to query policy rules
See AI Micro-Graphs showing citation proof for coverage decisions
Approve or deny claims up to financial threshold (e.g., $10,000 per claim)
Add notes and documentation to claim files
Restrictions:
Cannot upload new master policy rulebooks (IT Admin function)
Cannot modify user permissions or RBAC settings
Cannot view company-wide aggregate financial metrics (Manager function)
Cannot approve claims above their assigned dollar limit
Why This Role Exists: Sarah is the "Human-in-the-Loop" who verifies AI decisions before money is paid out, protecting the insurance company from AI hallucination liability.

Role 3: Claims Manager / Compliance Officer (Internal Business Leader)
Persona: Michael (Sarah's boss, Senior Compliance Officer)
Dashboard: PolicyMe Analytics Dashboard (Business intelligence focus)
Access Permissions:
View aggregate business metrics:
Total claims processed this month
AI approval rate vs. human override rate
Average processing time (target: <2 minutes)
Financial risk exposure by policy type
Override or review high-value claims (e.g., claims >$10,000)
Access "Conflict Console" showing policy contradictions detected by AI
Export compliance audit reports with full evidence trails
Assign claim quotas and performance targets to adjusters
Restrictions:
Cannot manage IT infrastructure or database configurations
Cannot configure system-level security settings or API integrations
Cannot access sensitive IT audit logs (separation of duties)
Why This Role Exists: Michael needs business intelligence to manage the claims department's performance and ensure regulatory compliance, but should not have access to technical infrastructure (SOC 2 compliance requirement).

Role 4: IT System Administrator (Internal Technical Support)
Persona: Dave (Geico's IT/Database Administrator)
Dashboard: PolicyMe Admin Control Panel (Utilitarian, technical interface)
Access Permissions:
Document Ingestion: Upload new 500-page master policy PDFs when company updates insurance products
User Management: Create employee accounts, assign RBAC roles (Adjuster, Manager, Admin)
System Health Monitoring: View API latency, Neo4j database performance, server uptime metrics
Security Audit Logs: See who logged in, when, and from which IP address
API Integration: Configure connections to customer portal, payment systems, HRIS
Restrictions (CRITICAL for Enterprise Compliance):
Cannot view financial details of individual claims (HIPAA/SOC 2 violation)
Cannot see customer medical records or personal injury details
Cannot approve or deny insurance claims (not a business user)
Why These Restrictions Exist: In enterprise architecture, IT staff manage infrastructure but must not have access to sensitive business/medical data. This enforces "Separation of Duties" required by compliance frameworks like SOC 2, HIPAA, and GDPR[7].

2.3 The Decoupled System Architecture
To satisfy enterprise security requirements, PolicyMe is built as a microservices architecture with clear separation between customer-facing and internal systems[9]:
Frontend Application A: Customer Portal (External)
Technology: React/Next.js (light-themed, consumer-friendly UI)
Users: John Doe (policyholders)
Features: Submit claims, upload photos, view claim status
Connection: Sends claim data via REST API to Backend Core System
Security: NO direct database access, NO AI access
Frontend Application B: InsurAI Command Center (Internal)
Technology: React/Next.js (dark-themed, enterprise dashboard)
Users: Sarah (Adjusters), Michael (Managers), Dave (IT Admins)
Features: Claims queue, AI Micro-Graphs, business analytics, document ingestion
Connection: Reads data from Backend Core System via authenticated API
Security: JWT authentication, role-based API endpoints
Backend: Core Intelligence Engine
Technology: Java Spring Boot + Python FastAPI + Neo4j Graph Database
Functions:
Receives claim from Customer Portal API
Triggers LangGraph Multi-Agent system to process claim
Queries Neo4j policy graph to determine coverage
Returns AI decision + citations to Command Center dashboard
Security: All business logic and data processing happens here, isolated from public internet
Why This Matters for Your Presentation:
When Infosys mentors ask, "Why didn't you just build one app where everyone logs in?" you answer:
"We built a decoupled microservices architecture following enterprise best practices. The Customer Portal is a lightweight engagement layer for consumers to submit claims. The Core Intelligence Engine is the internal 'System of Record' where the heavy GraphRAG processing happens. This separation ensures that if the customer-facing website crashes or gets DDoS attacked, our internal AI adjudication system remains secure and operational. We followed the same architectural pattern used by Fortune 500 insurance companies."
This proves you understand enterprise architecture, not just basic web development.

Step 4.2: Build GraphRAG Query Engine
Replit Agent Prompt:
Build a GraphRAG query processing microservice for PolicyMe.
Requirements:
Framework: Python with FastAPI
Endpoint: POST /graphrag/query
Input: { query: string, userId: string }
Process:
Generate query embedding using OpenAI embeddings API (text-embedding-3-small model)
Perform vector similarity search in Neo4j (find top 10 similar clauses)
Traverse graph to retrieve related clauses (multi-hop: 2 levels deep)
Construct context from retrieved clauses (max 4000 tokens)
Send context + query to OpenAI GPT-4 API with system prompt:
"You are PolicyMe, an AI assistant for corporate policy questions. Answer based only on provided context. Cite sources using [Policy Name § Clause ID] format."
Parse GPT-4 response to extract answer text and citations
Build micro-graph JSON: { nodes: [query, cited clauses], edges: [query→clause connections] }
Return: { answer: string, citations: Citation[], microGraph: GraphData, confidence: float }
Dependencies:
fastapi, uvicorn, openai, neo4j, numpy, langchain
Environment variables:
OPENAI_API_KEY, NEO4J_URI, NEO4J_USER, NEO4J_PASSWORD
Performance targets:
Vector search: <500ms
Graph traversal: <1s
Total response time: <8s
Add caching layer using Redis (cache query embeddings for 24 hours).
Expected Output: FastAPI microservice with GraphRAG pipeline.

5.3 Common Mentor Questions & Answers
Q: "Why not just use ChatGPT for claims processing?"
A: "ChatGPT hallucinates because it's a probabilistic model—it guesses the most likely answer based on training data, not facts. If a hospital files a $50,000 medical claim and ChatGPT hallucinates that it's covered when it's not, the insurance company gets sued. PolicyMe uses GraphRAG—it mathematically traverses a Neo4j knowledge graph. Every answer includes citations to specific policy clauses. If the answer isn't in the graph, the AI says 'Insufficient policy data' instead of making something up. That's why we can be used for actual financial decisions."
Q: "How do you prevent AI hallucinations specifically?"
A: "Three layers of protection: First, we use a closed-domain knowledge graph—the AI can only retrieve information from uploaded policy documents, never from general web training data. Second, our LangGraph agents implement a 'Retrieve → Verify → Cite' pipeline. The AI must cite the exact policy section for every claim. Third, we enforce a confidence threshold: if the AI's confidence score is below 85%, the claim is auto-routed to a senior human adjuster for manual review. We never let the AI make low-confidence decisions."
Q: "Why did you separate the customer portal from the internal system?"
A: "This is a standard enterprise architecture pattern called 'System of Engagement vs. System of Record.' The customer portal is just a lightweight data collection form—John Doe uploads his claim there. The PolicyMe Intelligence Engine is the 'System of Record' where the heavy AI processing happens. We decoupled them for three reasons: First, security—we can't expose our internal AI and database to the public internet where any customer could attack it. Second, liability—the AI never tells customers directly; a human adjuster verifies first. Third, compliance—sensitive medical and financial data must stay behind the enterprise firewall to meet HIPAA and SOC 2 requirements."
Q: "How do you handle data privacy and compliance?"
A: "PolicyMe is architected for enterprise compliance from day one. Enterprise data never leaves the customer's instance. We offer on-premise deployment with Docker/Kubernetes for carriers who require air-gapped infrastructure. For cloud deployment, we use AES-256 encryption at rest and TLS 1.3 in transit. We're SOC 2 Type II certified and HIPAA compliant for health insurance claims. All personally identifiable information (PII) is tokenized—claim data is stored separately from customer identity data. We also implement role-based access control so IT administrators cannot view claim financial details, enforcing separation of duties."
Q: "What's your business model and pricing?"
A: "B2B SaaS subscription sold to insurance carriers. Pricing tiers based on monthly claim volume: Starter tier $100K/year for up to 10,000 claims/month. Professional tier $300K/year for up to 50,000 claims/month. Enterprise tier custom pricing for carriers processing 100K+ claims/month. Revenue model is annual contract + 15% annual maintenance fee. The ROI is compelling: a mid-size carrier processing 20,000 claims/month currently spends $2M annually on adjuster salaries for manual processing. With PolicyMe, they reduce processing time by 90%, saving $1.8M/year. Our $300K subscription pays for itself in under 3 months."
Q: "How is this different from Guidewire or other insurance core systems?"
A: "Guidewire is a policy administration system—it manages billing, renewals, and data storage. It doesn't have AI intelligence. PolicyMe is an AI adjudication layer that sits on top of existing core systems. We integrate with Guidewire via API—when a claim comes in through Guidewire, it gets routed to PolicyMe for intelligent processing, then the decision flows back to Guidewire for payment execution. Think of it this way: Guidewire is the transaction engine, PolicyMe is the intelligence engine. Most insurance carriers already use Guidewire; they buy PolicyMe to add AI decision-making capabilities without replacing their existing infrastructure."
Q: "Why did you choose Neo4j instead of a traditional SQL database?"
A: "Insurance policies have recursive, many-to-many relationships that are impossible to query efficiently in SQL. For example, Policy A references Section 4.2, which references Addendum B, which modifies Section 7.3 in Policy C. In SQL, this requires 8-12 JOIN operations with exponential query complexity. In Neo4j, it's a single graph traversal query that executes in milliseconds. Graph databases are purpose-built for relationship-heavy data. When our AI needs to determine 'Does Policy X cover Situation Y?' it traverses the graph following edges labeled 'covers,' 'excludes,' 'modifies.' This is 100x faster than SQL for this specific use case. That's why Google, LinkedIn, and NASA use graph databases for knowledge systems."
Q: "What happens if the AI makes a wrong decision?"
A: "We have a Human-in-the-Loop override system. Every AI decision includes a confidence score (0-100%). If the score is below 85%, the claim is auto-flagged for human review. Additionally, all approved claims are logged with full audit trails. If a customer disputes a denial, the Claims Manager can review the AI's reasoning, see the exact policy citations, and override the decision if needed. All overrides are tracked in our analytics dashboard. If we detect a pattern of overrides (e.g., AI denied 10 windshield claims but humans approved all 10), the system alerts the IT Admin that the policy graph needs to be updated. This creates a feedback loop where the AI continuously improves."
Q: "How long does implementation take for a real insurance carrier?"
A: "Typical implementation: 8-12 weeks. Week 1-2: Data migration (upload existing policy PDFs, historical claims for AI training). Week 3-4: API integration with carrier's existing systems (Guidewire, Salesforce, payment processors). Week 5-6: User training (adjusters learn the interface, managers learn analytics dashboard). Week 7-8: Pilot phase with 10% of claims routed through PolicyMe while humans verify accuracy. Week 9-12: Full production rollout. We provide white-glove onboarding with dedicated implementation engineers."
Q: "Can this work for other industries beyond insurance?"
A: "Absolutely. The GraphRAG architecture applies to any domain with complex rulebooks: Banking (loan underwriting policies), Healthcare (treatment authorization policies), Legal (contract clause analysis), Government (regulatory compliance). The core technology is domain-agnostic. For this Infosys project, we specialized in insurance because it's a $5 trillion global market with a clear pain point. But the same system could process loan applications for JPMorgan or medical pre-authorizations for UnitedHealthcare with minimal code changes—just swap the policy documents and retrain the entity extraction models."
